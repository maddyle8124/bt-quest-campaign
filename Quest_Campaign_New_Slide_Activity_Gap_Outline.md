## Slide 3 (new) — The Activity Gap

**On-slide kicker:** The Activity Gap

**On-slide title:** Volume isn't activity. Activity isn't genuine activity.

**On-slide body — three-tier comparison, left to right:**

**Tier 1 — Volume**
- Short-window numbers. The chart moves, the headline figure gets posted, the screenshot circulates.
- No real person is tied to most of the candles. When the window closes, the volume goes with it.
- Chart shape: a flat baseline with periodic vertical spikes that decay back to the same baseline.

**Tier 2 — Activity**
- Real participants this time. People touch the token, click through the product, post in the channel.
- They came for the payout. Once it lands, they extract and leave. The chart returns to the prior baseline.
- Chart shape: a hill that grows and decays around each campaign, with no carryover between cycles.

**Tier 3 — Genuine activity**
- Sustained, distributed interaction from people rooted in the community.
- The same users return the next day and the next week. Holders stay. Trade frequency settles above the pre-campaign baseline.
- Chart shape: a step up in the baseline that does not reset when the campaign window closes.

**On-slide closing line (bridge into the next slide):**

Boosting native token activity is expensive. Most of what gets spent lands in Tier 1 or Tier 2. Genuine activity is what your token actually needs, and it is what our campaigns are designed to produce.

---

**Speaker notes:**

This is the perspective slide. Slide 2 listed three things we hear from token projects: marketing spend with no signal, a native token with no daily reason to be touched, and a community that is active but disconnected from trading and holding behavior. Those three are real, and they reinforce each other. Marketing reach without daily token interaction has no signal to amplify. A community without trading or holding behavior has nothing to point at on a chart. A token without an active user base has no story for marketing or community to tell. The loop runs in either direction, and naming any one of them as the root would be a simplification of how it actually behaves.

The reason we zoom in on the activity layer here is not that the other two do not matter. It is that this is the layer where a campaign can directly intervene. We do not run a token project's marketing team and we cannot manufacture a community from outside. We can structure incentives that produce real interaction with the native token, and when that interaction is genuine, the rest of the loop has something to work with.

The three-tier framing is the lens we use to read campaigns. Tier 1 is the easiest to manufacture and the first thing experienced buyers discount. A chart that shows a flat line with periodic vertical spikes signals a manufactured trail, not demand. Tier 2 is closer to what projects actually want, and is also where most reward-based approaches stop. Real people show up, claim, and leave. The chart looks healthier during the window and identical afterward. Tier 3 is harder to engineer, but it is the only tier that compounds. The same people coming back, holding, trading, using the product on day 30 the way they did on day 5.

If the client asks what falls into each tier, keep the answer structural rather than naming any specific method or platform. Tier 1 is what any short-window incentive without retention design produces: numbers without people. Tier 2 is what most reward-based campaigns deliver today: people who arrive for the reward and exit with it. Tier 3 is what our quest pack model is built to produce: client quests tied to repeated behavior, universal requirements that keep participants engaged through the full campaign window, and side quests that pull participants back beyond a single payout.

If the client raises cost (how much has already been spent on growth, how much more this will cost), the line to lean into is that current spend is paying for Tier 1 and Tier 2 repeatedly. The proposal we are bringing is not another spike on top. It is a redirect of budget toward the only tier that leaves something behind when the campaign closes.
